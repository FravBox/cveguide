# Community considerations

Acknowledge the traits that are specific to your community and how that will effect your event.

## Define the event

What exactly is your event? For the purposes of this document, we're assuming that your event is the following:

- Entrants submit videos
- All or most videos want critique or feedback (comments) of some kind
- Those same entrants will be doing the commenting
- There is a contest/awards component
- The entrants will be voting/judging for those awards
- (optional, but usually present) "mini-events" like activities or panels in voice calls

## Event's scale

How many people do you think will participate in your event?  
In an event that wants many thorough comments, the more videos there are, the harder that will be to achieve.

**Bigger number IS NOT better in this instance.**

Many people will feel pressure to comment on every video. If there are too many videos, this will backfire and people will give up before commenting on any of them.

You need to consider:

- limiting submissions in some way
- Having alternative ways of commenting/participating that lessen the cognitive load
- having different types of participants
  - Maybe some people only leave comments, and do not submit videos
  - Maybe some videos do not want comments
  - Perhaps there are staff whose sole job is to comment on things that don't have comments yet

## Identify your participants' dispositions

What type of person is the most likely to participate in your event?  
Is everyone trustworthy and friendly? Are they cut-throat and competitive? Are there trolls or people you should worry about?

Identify these traits in your community and keep them in mind for how you will deal with them.

### Security

If you have people who might cheat, you need to consider how to counter-act this. If your community doesn't have bad faith actors, this type of security can be put on the backburner while you work on other parts of your event.

For instance, if your users are the type to vote more than once, you need a method of ensuring unique vote limits.  
If anonymity is a core component of your event, you need ways to ensure that anonymity is present for the event participants while coordinators & staff still have access to their identities if you have trolls.

In our specific event, you may need to be worried about non-participants participating. The default setting of the kollab links is technically public and can be shared with anyone, who will be able to comment. If your users are prone to sharing outside the circle, you need a method to deal with this case as well.

### Rules

Not all communities can intuit what the event's participation should feel like. Some types of communities need to be told what they can and cannot do explicitly. Moreover, some communities may need specific types of rules that other communities won't.

If your community is full of highly competitive people, you may have to create some rules around "talking down" videos for a competitive edge or about peer pressuring people to vote certain ways. Other communities might need rules around what types of critique or discussions are acceptable.

If your community has users with specialized needs, those needs should be considered - e.g. all videos should include captions, file size restrictions (for bad internet connections or limited hard drive space), etc.

### Moderation / staffing

Each event needs some type of moderation. The jobs of these people will vary depending on your community's needs. Some communities may need staff who will actually enforce rules - outright warning people, preventing arguments, dictating punishments, etc.

But other communities may need mods in roles like starting discussions, holding panels, hyping people up, or just being available for answering frequent questions.

In my opinion, **moderation is the most important determinant of an event's success.**

Users trained or experienced in the event can overcome obstacles like unclear rules, improper (official) communication, and/or disorganization.  
They can act as community leaders even when your community is friendly and has no official ruleset. They can initiate discussions, comments, or mini-events, which is especially helpful in communities where social anxieties is present.

This is in addition to the more obvious duties like stopping drama or performing damage control.

Good moderators can save a failing or dead event and make participants feel valued. Likewise, bad moderators can drag down an otherwise well-planned event and discourage participants. An off-color comment hurts a lot more when it is coming from a moderator versus a regular participant.

**Give your moderators the tools necessary to deal with your specific community's needs**
Some examples:

- familiarity/training with how the event works, so they can answer questions
- actual tools for damage control (ability to move/mute/kick users from voice chat, initiate channel slowmode, etc.)
- a staffing schedule so mini-events have official staff coverage
- be able to break blind (if necessary) to deal with backend event things

Again, what is necessary depends on what type of community disposition you have and the exact flavor of the event you're running.

In some communities, moderators can be participants in the event without issue. Other communities will not view this favorably and must have a staff that is not involved with the participation side of the event.

Staffing might need to be a team or in some communities just one person. Every community's needs are different and even the flavor of the event can change the needs.

### Schedule

If your community is known for participating in particular events, you will want to schedule your event so you are not directly competing for those dates. Likewise, if your users are prone to burn out, you may want to schedule your event in the middle of a dead period instead of near others.

If you have a part of your community that often participates in one thing and another part of your community that doesn't, you could cater your event to the side that has nothing to do during that time period. This would share the dates without directly competing.

In the case of our type of event - which has multiple phases of voting - you need to consider how long your users will take to watch the videos and participate in each phase. Some communities may take a long time to watch videos and a short time to vote or deliberate. Other communities may need more or less time elsewhere.

## Accessibility

Accessibility is more than content warnings (CWs) and/or thinking about disabled people. It is anything that makes it easier for people to access your event.

Clear communication is accessibility.  
Alternate methods of participation (e.g. text vs voice comments) are accessibility.  
A published schedule of events is accessibility.  
See [the curb cut effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curb_cut_effect).

**Always assume this is the first time someone is interacting with your event.** You shouldn't rely on "common knowledge."

Have staff/mods on standby to explain how the event works and answer any questions and/or have a knowledgebase somewhere.

### Event information / reference

In my specific case, I have a static webpage that lists all the rules, the schedule (which is the same every year), and some FAQs. This makes it easier for me (or anyone) to point to for easily answering questions. Interested parties can also look up the answers themselves.

I've mirrored this information into discord posts as well for people who don't want to navigate to a website.

I think having this information publicly accessible is valuable for those who want to research before joining. It's also faster to link a site than explain how each piece works every year to someone.

However, your community may require personal conversations or you may simply like to explain. In this case, it's still helpful to have some type of static reference point, but it may not be as important as having knowledgeable people available to talk about the event.

I am biased, but I am hesitant to join any event I can't read up about beforehand. I want my facts from the source rather than passed through the grapevine, especially for events which have multiple phases like ours. Being able to look at the event's information also helps prevent me from feeling like I sound stupid if I'm new.

Having a static information page helps with event promotion, expectations, and social anxiety.

How the information is structured also depends on your community's needs. Some communities want paragraphs of explanation. Others may only want a list of links.

Specific examples:

- static webpage with rules etc
- separate discord channel with important links and nothing else
- emailing participants information in addition to discord messages
- pinging users for updates

### Discord pings

**Avoid pinging everyone.** Each time you ping @everyone, at least one person will mute all notifications and it will make disseminating your information that much harder.

It only takes one ping at a bad time for a user to check out from your event and never look back at the channel/server.

**When every update gets a ping, no update is important.**  
Most communities would be served better by intentional pings like roles people sign up to willingly.

For our specific event, I've found the best method to be a separate news channel where only "important" news items get posted.  
I only ping for news items that require user response, like a new voting form opening.

Some communities may want more or less pings, e.g. deadline reminders or mini-events.

### Pinning messages

Pinned messages are useful if you want to point people to a specific important message frequently. **In most cases, only the most-recently pinned message is looked at.**

It's important to note that:

- pinned messages are difficult to view on mobile
- sometimes pinned messages won't load at all on mobile
- pinned messages may as well not exist in threads on mobile
- most people don't think to look at pinned messages

If the information you wish to convey is very short, you may be better served by editing the channel topic instead of pinning a message. A channel topic will show as a subtitle/caption to a channel name at the top of the window on PC, in the browse channel menu on all platforms, and when tapping the channel name on all platforms.  
Note that markdown (discord formatting) won't work in a channel topic.

**Pinned messages must be understandable __without__ context!**

If you have 2 messages:

1. Who's interested in my mini-event?
2. sign up here! \[link]

And you pin message 2, understand that everyone who looks at this pin will have no idea what the link is for. This makes that pin useless.

Create a new message with all the information necessary to understand it, or edit your pinned message.

Fixed example:

1. Sign up for my event about XYZ here: \[link]

**More than ~3 pinned messages needs its own reference channel.**  
If you have important messages in the pins, but people can't find them quickly, no one will look in the pins. A channel is easier to navigate and search.

Create a separate channel to store this information, understand that every message is not pin-worthy, or create a new single pinned message that links to all the other messages so they are easier to find.

### Mini-events

Generally speaking, people need to be prompted to make their own mini-events.  
Rely on community leaders to break the ice by hosting the first event and/or suggesting ideas other users would be able to host.  
Additionally, make the messaging incredibly clear that anyone can host any type of mini-event to encourage participation.

Getting people to make mini-events is only half the battle, though. You also need people to participate in those mini-events. A host without any attendees will likely not host again.

To that end, consider:

- available times people can participate (your community's majority timezones)
- clarity of event messages (is the date/time/description easily found?)
- appropriate advance notice (do people know the event will happen before it has already started?)

Some communities will need more advanced notice than others and/or have the mini-event start time displayed in multiple timezones. Some communities will require a moderator present for all mini-events. Others may require certain information about the mini-events be present in the description at creation time.

Here's what I personally prefer:

- separate mini-events channel with a list of upcoming mini-events
- voluntary ping role for new event created and 5 minutes before the event starts
- a thread for each event (the host can give info to all attendees and for historical reasons)
- event start time uses a [unix timestamp](https://hammertime.cyou). (Desktop discord has an `@time` function that will also accomplish this.)
- mini-event description must convey some specific information
  - what channel the mini-event will occur in (my server has multiple)
  - what the event actually is (will it have critique, what are we talking about, etc.)
  - if disability accommodations are provided. If they are, what & how are they being provided? (Accommodations are not required, but stating that in the description is. My community has certain accessibility needs other communities may not.)

You can accomplish these in ways like:

- discord bots
- a specific format people copy & paste
- moderators create the notices for the mini-event hosts in the proper format/area

### Methods of contact

How do people ask questions? Who should they ask the questions to? Who is allowed to answer those questions?

Some communities need a special channel for questions where only moderators can answer. Other communities may allow a more free-form approach. If you have users that like to DM questions, tell them who they should be DMing or if they need to post publicly instead.

Have a list of moderators, community leaders, and/or coordinators who are available to be contacted or who can be trusted to answer questions correctly.

This can be an actual list in a channel, reference message, or on the website. It could also be as simple as a role icon, badge, name color, or specific naming scheme like `[Mod] MyName`.

In a context where the accuracy of the answers matter, the official source of those answers should be distinguished in some way.

Additionally, how do people report when things go wrong? Should they DM someone? Is there a `/report` command? Do your users know how to use it? This information should be conveyed to the event participants.

## Marketing, outreach, & community culture

This page assumes you're creating an event for a pre-existing discord server community.

Before advertising outside your community to attract more participants, remember what your community is like, its disposition, and the definition of your event.

Don't outreach to people or advertise in spaces that are too different from your current community and culture unless you are explicitly trying to change it.

Inviting too many people of different dispositions from your current community can cause discomfort and alienate both the old and new participants.

What this means in practice varies.    
Let's assume your community primarily edits anime music videos.

If you reach out to live-action music video editors and they participate in your event, they are a minority. The anime editors might not rate their videos as highly as the AMVs and thus not have a fair chance at winning any awards. They may be unblinded by simple process of elimination since no one in your community edits non-anime.

The live-action editors may be dissuaded from returning in the future, and the anime editors who don't want to watch live-action videos may also leave.

This can happen even without any intentional negativity.

Also consider if your outreach is a bit too successful - and the live-action editors outnumber the anime ones. Now your original community may feel like they're being stamped out and you're changing the expectations of the event from underneath them.

This is an over-simplified example. Most community culture clash will be far more minor, like editors with certain identifiable styles, extreme timezone variance(if they can't be online when everyone else is active, that's alienating), and communication differences.

How to counter the feeling of alienation from outreach also varies.

If you're intentionally trying to change the community, it's mostly a matter of pacing the changes over a longer time period and accepting some losses.

During the transition period (which will likely be multiple events), you'll want to try make each group feel valued. In our example, you'll probably want to offer incentives for live-action videos, like a dedicated category, or change a video submission rule like you get to submit an extra video if it is live-action.
